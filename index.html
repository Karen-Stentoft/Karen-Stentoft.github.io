Introduction

In recent years, San Francisco has frequently been mentioned in discussions about the opioid and fentanyl crisis in the United States. Media coverage and public debate often point to a growing problem with drug use and drug-related activity in the city. But to what extent is this reflected in official crime data?

In this analysis, we use police records from the city of San Francisco covering the period from 2003 to 2025. The data is obtained from the official San Francisco open data portal and allows us to examine how registered drug-related offenses have evolved over time.

We focus on identifying long-term trends, geographical patterns across the city, and temporal patterns in the data, in order to better understand whether recent developments differ from earlier years.

Data

The data used in this analysis originates from the official records of the city of San Francisco, available through the open data portal. Two datasets have been combined: one covering the period from 2003 to 2018 and another from 2018 to 2025. Some preprocessing steps were applied to ensure consistency across the datasets.

The development of drug offenses in San Francisco

We begin by examining how registered drug-related offenses have developed over time in San Francisco.

To provide context, we compare drug offenses with a selection of other crime categories, referred to as the "focused crime categories". This allows us to assess whether changes in drug-related incidents follow broader crime trends or deviate from them.

The figure below shows the development of these categories from 2003 to 2025, with drug offenses highlighted.

Overall, most crime categories show a declining trend in recent years. In contrast, drug-related offenses exhibit a different pattern, with a noticeable increase over the same period.

This divergence suggests that drug offenses do not simply follow the general development of crime in the city, but instead represent a distinct and evolving trend.

Geographical distribution of drug offenses

To investigate the spatial patterns of drug-related offenses, we visualize the density of incidents across San Francisco using an interactive heatmap. The map allows us to explore how these patterns evolve over time.

The distribution of drug offenses is not uniform across the city. Instead, incidents are highly concentrated in specific areas, indicating the presence of persistent hotspots.

This spatial concentration highlights that drug-related activity is localized rather than widespread, and that changes in overall trends are closely tied to a limited number of high-density areas. Over time, the distribution of drug-related offenses becomes increasingly concentrated in central areas of the city, particularly around the Tenderloin district. While earlier years show a more dispersed pattern with activity present in areas such as Bayview and Richmond, recent data suggests that incidents are becoming more localized in a smaller number of high-density hotspots.

Development of crime through the day

To further explore the patterns in drug-related offenses, we examine how incidents are distributed across the hours of the day. The interactive plot shows the number of recorded offenses for each hour, allowing us to compare patterns across years.

A clear daily rhythm is observed, with lower activity during early morning hours and higher levels later in the day. This indicates that drug-related offenses are not evenly distributed throughout the day, but follow a consistent temporal pattern.

While the overall number of incidents increases in recent years, the shape of the distribution remains largely unchanged. This suggests that the rise in drug offenses is not driven by new temporal patterns, but rather by an intensification of existing activity during the same hours of the day.
